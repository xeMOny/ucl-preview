PhD in Law and Criminology

The PhD in Law and Criminology is an advanced academic degree focused on exploring the legal system, criminal behaviour and society's responses to crime.

Advanced Legal Theory

10 ECTS

This module examines the main jurisprudential approaches. It begins with an analysis of legal positivism's conception of law as a system of social rules, followed by an analysis of legal realism's focus on judicial behaviour and decision-making. Critical theories — Marxist, feminist and postcolonial — are then applied to reveal how law can both reflect and reinforce existing power structures. The module concludes with a normative analysis designed to evaluate the ethical foundations of legal institutions and practices, addressing the nature of law and its justified purposes.

Advanced Criminological Theory

10 ECTS

This module presents a comprehensive analysis of contemporary theories of criminality. It examines the classical rational choice and deterrence models before exploring positivist explanations rooted in biological and psychological factors. Sociological perspectives — strain, social disorganisation, social learning and labelling — are then assessed in relation to economic inequality. Critical frameworks follow, interrogating the roles of power and ideology in defining and responding to crime. The module concludes with a normative and policy analysis of punishment, rehabilitation and restorative justice, evaluating their theoretical foundations and social impacts.

Legal and Criminological Research Methodology

10 ECTS

This module provides a complete introduction to qualitative and quantitative research methodologies applied to legal and criminological research. It covers the formulation of research questions and hypotheses, the design of empirical and doctrinal research, and the use of systematic data-collection strategies — including interviews, case studies and surveys — while also addressing sampling techniques, statistical methods and data visualisation. Participants will gain hands-on experience with industry-standard software (such as SPSS and NVivo) for processing and analysing quantitative datasets, and will learn to build clear, evidence-based arguments. The module emphasises ethical principles — privacy, informed consent and data integrity — to ensure that all research is conducted to the highest standards of rigour and responsibility.

Human Rights and Criminal Justice

10 ECTS

This module examines the international and national human rights architecture and its intersection with criminal justice systems. It begins by examining foundational treaties and conventions — such as the Universal Declaration of Human Rights, the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights and the European Convention on Human Rights — together with their corresponding enforcement mechanisms. The course then explores how these instruments shape procedural safeguards and substantive limits on state power, with particular attention to the prohibition of torture, children's rights, judicial oversight of detention, fair trial and due process rights, and the availability of effective remedies.

Criminal Policy and Public Security

10 ECTS

This module offers a complete examination of criminal policy and public security, tracing the development, implementation and evaluation of strategies aimed at crime prevention and citizen protection. It begins with a review of foundational theories — rational choice, social prevention and situational prevention — and proceeds to examine crime-control models such as environmental design, community policing and restorative justice. The ethical, legal and social dimensions of security policy — civil liberties, proportionality and legitimacy — are integrated throughout the course to ensure a full understanding of how public policy shapes and responds to crime.

International Criminal Law

10 ECTS

This module provides a rigorous examination of the foundations, content and application of international criminal law. It begins by defining and distinguishing the core international crimes — genocide, war crimes, crimes against humanity and the crime of aggression — within the framework of the key treaties (e.g. the Genocide Convention and the Geneva Conventions) and customary norms. The module then focuses on the establishment, jurisdiction and procedures of the International Criminal Court under the Rome Statute, together with the work of the ad hoc tribunals (e.g. ICTY, ICTR) and hybrid courts.

Cybercrime

10 ECTS

This online module offers a comprehensive examination of cybercrime, exploring the full spectrum of illicit activities in digital environments — from phishing and financial fraud to identity theft, ransomware attacks and online harassment. It examines the international frameworks governing cybercrime, including the Budapest Convention and EU directives, and analyses how courts and law enforcement adapt traditional principles of jurisdiction, evidence and procedure to cyberspace. The course also introduces key investigative tools and techniques — computer forensics, network tracing, malware analysis — and addresses operational challenges such as attribution, encryption and cross-border cooperation.

Feminist Criminology

10 ECTS

This module offers a rigorous examination of feminist perspectives in criminology, exploring how gender shapes experiences of crime, victimisation and justice. It introduces the key feminist theories — liberal, radical, Marxist and intersectional — and their applications to the study of gender-based violence, female offending and barriers to accessing justice. Through critical analysis of case law, regulatory frameworks and empirical research, students will evaluate institutional responses to intimate partner violence, sexual assault and female incarceration, and examine gender-sensitive alternatives such as restorative justice and legal reform.

Comparative Law

10 ECTS

This module offers a comparative examination of the world's legal traditions — common law, civil law, Islamic law and hybrid systems — and their approaches to criminal justice, human rights and the administration of justice. Starting from an overview of each system's historical foundations and institutional structures, the course then analyses how they define and punish crime, protect individual rights and guarantee due process. Through case studies from multiple jurisdictions, students will explore variations in substantive criminal law, procedural safeguards and philosophies of punishment, as well as the influence of international norms on domestic practice.

Doctoral Thesis Preparation and Defence

120 ECTS

The core of the PhD in Law and Criminology is the doctoral thesis: an original piece of research that must contribute to knowledge in one or more fields of law or criminology. Each candidate will select either law or criminology as the main disciplinary lens for their thesis, allowing depth of focus while maintaining the program's interdisciplinary foundation. The process includes: definition of the research topic (in collaboration with the thesis supervisor, an original topic that adds significant value to the field); fieldwork or documentary analysis (interviews, surveys and case studies, or review of case law, legislation and theories); thesis writing (rigorous academic format, with a well-defined theoretical framework, clear methodology and conclusions based on the results); and the thesis defence before a committee of experts, with a presentation of the work and a thorough analysis of the research findings and implications. The thesis will demonstrate advanced research competence, critical analysis and a contribution to knowledge consistent with MQF Level 8 standards.
